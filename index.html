Points reward the sweet spot: a sensible word the machine ranked low. The obvious word scores nothing — that is its own cliché.     How a wager is scored

    Your word is read as a probability: how likely the machine thought it was, given the line so far. That probability is turned into "surprise." Too little surprise means you picked the obvious word. Too much means the machine found your word barely possible at all — the mark of nonsense. Points peak between the two, where a word is unexpected yet still probable enough to be sensible.

    Why nonsense cannot win

    This is the honest heart of the game. A meter that simply rewarded surprise would crown gibberish champion, because to a small machine a nonsense word and a brilliant one are equally startling. By paying only in the middle band, the scoring itself refuses the trick — no disclaimer required. A truly wild guess is named "wild," not "genius."

    What "sensible" really means here

    The machine does not judge sense; it judges probability, which is only a proxy for sense. A moderately probable word is usually coherent, not always. Treat a high score as "the machine found this a plausible surprise," not as proof the word was good. That final judgement stays with the player.

    The honest caveat

    A 360-million-parameter model, tuned to sound like an assistant, sets these odds — so its notion of the "expected" word is rough and a little assistant-flavoured. The great models would bet differently. Read the game as a mirror for your own reflexes, not a ruling. Nothing typed here leaves the browser.
